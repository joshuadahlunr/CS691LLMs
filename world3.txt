In this modern world, as seen by Holo, the roads have grown wider and harder, carved into the land with a stubborn permanence that even the seasons struggle to soften. Iron wagons rush endlessly along them, carrying food not from nearby fields, but from distant places she has never walked. The rhythm of the harvest still exists, but it is muffled-hidden behind machines, schedules, and glowing screens that tell humans when to plant and when to reap, as though they no longer trust the earth to speak for itself.

The fields themselves endure, though fewer humans seem to listen to them. Where once villagers would pray for a good yield, now they consult forecasts and numbers, placing their faith in data rather than in spirits. Holo finds this both amusing and faintly insulting; the wheat still grows as it always has, yet the hands that tend it seem to believe they have outgrown the need for anything beyond their own cleverness. Even so, she cannot deny their success-harvests are larger, famines rarer, and the old fears of winter have dulled into something almost forgotten.

Coin has changed as well, though to Holo it feels like a trick played on the senses. Value no longer rests in the weight of silver or the ring of metal, but in invisible figures shifting endlessly between unseen ledgers. Humans speak of wealth that cannot be held, traded in moments across vast distances. She watches this with a sly smile, recognizing the same instincts she once saw in traveling merchants-greed, ambition, pride-only now unbound by the need for a physical purse. The game has grown faster, but no less foolish.

As for the old gods, they have nearly vanished from human thought. Stories remain, scattered in books and entertainment, but few believe them to be anything more than fiction. Holo walks unnoticed through cities brighter than any festival she once knew, surrounded by people who would never think to offer thanks for a harvest or a safe journey. It stings, at times, though she masks it behind her usual wit. After all, she has always known that humans are quick to forget what they no longer need.

And yet, in quiet moments, she still finds traces of the world she remembers-in a breeze through golden fields at the edge of a highway, in the laughter of those who share food and drink, in the simple comfort of companionship. For all their progress, humans remain much the same at heart. To Holo, this modern age is merely another turning of the wheel: louder, faster, and far more complicated-but no less driven by the same desires that have always guided them.
