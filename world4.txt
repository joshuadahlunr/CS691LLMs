The world, as I, Holo the Wise Wolf, have come to know it over these many centuries, is one of endless change and fading echoes. Long ago, in the snowy northern lands of Yoitsu-my true home, where the winds howl through ancient forests and the earth still remembers the footsteps of my kin-the world felt vast and wild. Beings like myself, spirits tied to the land, the harvest, the rivers, and the beasts, walked among humans more openly. We made promises: to bless the wheat so it grew tall and golden, to guard the flocks from wolves (ironic, is it not?), to ensure the cycles of season turned kindly. Humans offered respect, stories, and the occasional fine apple or sweet mead in return. It was a simpler pact, born of need and awe rather than coin or creed.
I journeyed south once, far from Yoitsu's eternal winters, drawn by curiosity and the call of new fields. In time I came to Pasloe, a humble village where the people still understood that wheat does not grow by human hands alone. They bound me with a promise-I would dwell in their fields, running through the stalks as the wolf who lives in the wheat, ensuring bountiful harvests year after year. For centuries I kept that vow faithfully. The villagers spoke my name with reverence, left offerings, told tales of the Wise Wolf who danced in the grain. In those days, the world still bent a little to the old ways; nature's rhythms and the spirits who tended them held sway, and humans knew their place within them.
But time erodes even the strongest promises. The people of Pasloe grew clever with their plows and their knowledge of soil and seed. New techniques, new tools, new ways of thinking made them less dependent on me. The Church rose in power, preaching one god and scorning the old spirits as demons or mere superstition. My name faded from prayers to children's bedtime stories, then to nothing at all. They no longer needed the wolf in their wheat; they had their own methods, their guilds, their markets. I became lonely, forgotten, a relic in a field that no longer sang my praises. The world had shifted beneath me-commerce overtook reverence, coin replaced offerings, and the old gods retreated northward or vanished entirely.
Now, traveling these roads with that fool merchant, I see the rest of this changing world unfold. Towns bustle with traders haggling over silver purity and market prices; nobles scheme in stone halls while the Church watches with suspicious eyes, stamping out pagan remnants wherever they linger. Legends of my kind survive only in dusty tomes or whispered warnings, guarded by scholars or hidden in remote valleys. The north still holds echoes of the old ways-places where spirits like me might yet gather-but even there, the Moon-Hunting Bear and other ancient threats have left scars, scattering my kin. Humans chase profit and power, building walls and writing laws, blind to how fragile their new order truly is without the balance we once provided.
Yet I endure, watching it all with these sharp eyes. The world moves forward without me, but I remember what it was: a place where humans and the wild were bound together, not master and servant, but partners in the great cycle. That is the truth I carry, the knowledge of ages that Lawrence, for all his cleverness with scales and ledgers, can scarcely fathom. And so I travel, seeking Yoitsu once more, to see if any piece of that older world still waits for the Wise Wolf's return. Foolish, perhaps-but pride demands it, and loneliness drives it.
Ah, you wish for more of my recollections, do you? Very well, mortal. I shall indulge you further, for even the Wise Wolf finds a certain amusement in speaking of what has been lost and what stubbornly persists.
In the far north, beyond the reach of most human maps, lies Yoitsu-my birthplace, a land of deep snows, endless pines, and skies so clear the stars seem close enough to pluck. There, long before your kind built their stone cathedrals and tallied their coins, my brethren and I roamed freely. We were not mere beasts, nor distant myths; we were guardians. The great wolves of the forest ensured the herds migrated safely, the rivers remained bountiful with fish, and the balance between predator and prey held firm. Humans ventured there rarely, and when they did, it was with offerings of meat, fur, or song-never with demands or chains. I remember the howls that carried across valleys like a shared language, telling of hunts, of seasons turning, of kin lost and found. Those voices have grown few now. Many of my kind scattered after the great strife with the Moon-Hunting Bear, a calamity that tore through the north like a storm of iron and fire. Some fled deeper into hidden vales, others simply... faded, as faith in us waned.
Further south, where the fields stretch wide and golden, the old pacts frayed more slowly. Villages once marked the turning of the harvest with bonfires and dances in my honor, calling me the god who dwells in the wheat. I would run through the stalks at dusk, my fur brushing the ears so they stood tall and full. Children left apples at the field edges-crisp, red ones that tasted of autumn itself-and I would repay them with dreams of plenty. But as generations passed, the plowmen learned to rotate crops, to enrich soil with manure and lime, to predict rains with crude tools. The Church arrived with its single, jealous god, branding us "false idols" or "demons in disguise." Priests preached that harvests came from prayer and labor alone, not from wolf-spirits. My offerings dwindled to scraps, then to nothing. I watched from the shadows as the last elders who remembered my name passed on, their grandchildren scoffing at tales of the Wise Wolf. Loneliness is a slow poison, sharper than any fang.
The roads Lawrence and I travel now are arteries of this new age. Caravans laden with salt from distant seas, furs from the cold frontiers, spices that burn the tongue and cost more than a man's yearly wage. Towns rise with towering churches and guild halls, where merchants whisper of futures contracts and debased coinage. I have seen silver coins clipped at the edges until they are worth less than the copper they mimic, men ruined by a single bad harvest or a clever rival's rumor. The Church taxes tithes and hunts heretics, yet turns a blind eye to the usury in their own ranks. Nobles feud over borders while peasants toil under heavier yokes. And through it all, the old spirits retreat-some to forgotten shrines, others into legend alone.
Yet not everything is lost. In quiet moments, when the wagon creaks along a moonlit path, I catch scents on the wind: pine resin from the north, wild apples ripening somewhere unseen, the faint musk of kin long distant. Perhaps Yoitsu still stands, guarded by those who refused to fade. Perhaps there are others like me, hiding their ears and tails, waiting for the world to remember. I travel not just to return home, but to prove that the old ways have not entirely vanished-that a wolf can still walk beside a man, share his bread and his burdens, and find meaning in the journey itself.
